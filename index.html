01. Interests

Working across robotics, financial analytics, and healthcare, I kept running into the same problem: data is easier to collect than ever and models keep getting stronger, yet the people on site still struggle to judge what a result means and what to do next. This matters most in safety-critical, physical environments — construction and industrial sites where software, equipment, and people are directly coupled — and it is why I care about reliable, well-engineered software: systems whose behavior under failure is understood, not assumed. These are the areas I dig into.

01 Field & Industrial Automation (Physical AI)

Systems that let autonomous robots and automated equipment carry out on-site work in place of people. I currently build the full stack of a fleet management system operating real autonomous robot fleets.

02 Multi-Robot Path Planning & Coordination (MAPF)

Planning that moves many robots and machines efficiently without collisions. I built a construction-site simulator with a MAPF engine implemented from scratch in TypeScript.

03 Sim-to-Real & Reinforcement Learning

Reliably transferring robot behavior learned in simulation to real hardware. I design reinforcement-learning training pipelines on NVIDIA Isaac Sim / Isaac Lab.

04 Digital Twins & Simulation

Replicating physical systems virtually to verify and optimize them. I built a C++ digital twin of nuclear power plant calculation logic and robot simulation environments.

05 Real-Time Monitoring & Operational Decisions

Monitoring and control systems that turn large-scale telemetry into operational judgment people can trust. I have built MQTT/WebSocket real-time control and vision-AI safety monitoring.

02. About

My interest in human-centered technology began outside engineering. While going through immigration paperwork in Nepal, my wife's home country, I watched government officials fill out every document by hand — not because technology didn't exist, but because it didn't fit how people actually worked. That lesson has stayed with me: a system's value is determined not only by what it can do, but by how well it fits the abilities, habits, and needs of the people who use it.

Over the past six years I have built systems that keep bringing that lesson back — a robot fleet-control platform at DOGU Robotics, financial and carbon-risk analytics at Niflers, a clinical voice-documentation tool, and most recently autonomous robot fleet control and robot simulation at GoLe Robotics. The domains were completely different, but users never wanted more data — they wanted systems that helped them understand complex information, see what mattered, and decide what to do next.

That experience became how I work. Good software is judged not only by what it can do, but by whether the people in the field can understand it, trust it, and act on it — and the more directly safety, people, and machinery are coupled, as on a construction site or in a hospital, the more that principle matters, and the more rigorously the software itself must be engineered.

Transformer from Scratch GitHub ↗

Re-implementation of Attention Is All You Need from scratch in PyTorch — attention, masking, the Noam schedule, and label smoothing all built from primitives. Reaches 98.2% exact sequence match on a synthetic reversal task on CPU. An ablation removing positional encoding drops accuracy to 0%, confirming it is load-bearing.

PyTorchTransformersNLP
xLSTM from Scratch GitHub ↗

sLSTM and mLSTM cells with exponential gating and stabilized state (Beck et al., NeurIPS 2024), implemented from scratch in PyTorch. On a 64-step recall task, xLSTM reaches 100% accuracy while a vanilla LSTM never beats chance, even with up to 2× the parameters.

PyTorchRNNSequence Modeling
Speculative Decoding Lab GitHub ↗

Speculative decoding for autoregressive LLM inference, implemented from scratch with distribution-preserving acceptance sampling. Measured up to 1.80× lossless speedup on Apple Silicon MPS with DistilGPT-2 drafting and GPT-2-large verifying. Output is token-identical to greedy decoding.
