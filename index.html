01. Research Interests

Across robotics, financial analytics, and healthcare, I keep running into the same gap: data is easier than ever to collect and models are increasingly capable, yet users still struggle to interpret what an answer means and decide what to do next. The research I hope to pursue in graduate study asks how intelligent systems can help people move from information to action.

01 Intelligent Decision-Support Systems

Systems that help users reason through complex, domain-specific information and reach sound decisions — not just surface more data.

02 Human–AI Collaboration

AI that augments human judgment rather than replacing it, keeping people in control of consequential decisions.

03 Domain-Specific AI

Bringing specialized knowledge and context — in finance, healthcare, and operations — into a model's reasoning, where general-purpose answers fall short.

04 Explainability, Trust & Uncertainty

Helping systems communicate how confident they are and why, so people know when information is reliable enough to act on.

05 Robotics Simulation & Sim-to-Real

Learning robot behaviors in simulation through reinforcement learning and foundation models, then transferring them reliably to the physical world.

02. About

My interest in human-centered technology began outside of engineering. While navigating immigration and administrative procedures in Nepal — my spouse's home country — I watched officials fill out forms by hand, not because the technology was missing, but because it didn't fit how people actually worked. The lesson stayed with me: a system's value depends not only on what it can do, but on whether it fits the abilities, habits, and needs of the people who use it.

Over the past five years I have built systems where that lesson kept resurfacing — robot monitoring platforms at Dogu Robotics, financial and carbon-risk analytics at Niflers, a clinical speech-to-text tool, and, most recently, autonomous-robot fleet management and robotics simulation at Gole Robotics. Across very different domains, users rarely wanted more data; they wanted help understanding complex information, seeing what mattered, and deciding what to do next.

That recurring pattern became the question I want to study: how can intelligent systems transform complex domain information into knowledge people can understand, trust, and act upon?
